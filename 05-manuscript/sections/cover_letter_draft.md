Dear Editors,

We submit for your consideration our manuscript entitled "When building cities destroys value: a persistent regime shift in urban investment across nations." This study introduces Urban Q -- the ratio of urban asset value to replacement cost -- as a diagnostic framework for detecting when urban investment crosses from value-creating to value-destroying. Our central finding is that China's marginal Urban Q turned negative in 2022 (p = 0.043): each additional unit of investment now reduces, rather than increases, the total value of the urban asset stock. This transition is embedded in a broader structural shift confirmed by Bai-Perron break tests (F = 30.1, p < 0.0001), with 98.8% of Monte Carlo paths -- propagating uncertainty across seven independent measurement calibrations -- falling below Q = 1 before 2025.

The implications extend well beyond China's economy. We estimate that China's cumulative overbuilding has generated approximately 13.4 GtCO2 in embodied construction emissions since 2000, with annual peaks reaching 11.8% of national total carbon output -- making overbuilding one of the largest underrecognised sources of avoidable emissions globally. Across 52 countries whose capital-price ratios declined more than 20% from peak, 67.3% exhibited rising first-order autocorrelation in the preceding decade (binomial test p = 0.009), a signature of critical slowing down that suggests the regime shift we document in China reflects a general dynamical pattern of urban investment transitions in developing economies. These findings provide actionable early warning for India, Vietnam, Indonesia, and other rapidly urbanising nations where construction-led growth is accelerating toward similar thresholds.

Our analysis draws on a 158-country panel (World Bank, Penn World Table, BIS property prices) covering 1960-2019 and a 290-city panel for China with real fixed-asset investment data. We address the fundamental challenge of measuring unobservable urban asset values through a calibre uncertainty framework that constructs seven independent value-cost ratios and propagates their disagreement via Monte Carlo simulation, delivering a robust directional conclusion despite substantial uncertainty in precise timing. To our knowledge, this is the first study to operationalise Tobin's Q for the urban built environment at national and cross-national scale, the first to quantify overbuilding's embodied carbon cost, and the first to document early-warning signals for urban investment regime shifts across a global sample.
