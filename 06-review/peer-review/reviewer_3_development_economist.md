# Reviewer Report: Development Economics & Public Investment Perspective

**Paper**: "Simpson's paradox masks declining returns on urban investment worldwide"
**Target journal**: Nature (main journal)
**Reviewer expertise**: Development economics, public investment efficiency, institutional economics, China's economy
**Review date**: 2026-03-21

---

## 1. Overall Assessment

This paper introduces "Marginal Urban Q" (MUQ) -- the incremental asset value per unit of investment -- as a metric for urban investment efficiency, and documents three empirical patterns: a Simpson's paradox in global investment returns, a sign reversal in the MUQ-investment intensity relationship between China and the US, and a carbon cost estimate for below-unity investment in China. The ambition is considerable, and the paper engages with an important question at the intersection of development economics, urban economics, and climate policy.

However, from the standpoint of the development economics literature on investment efficiency, I have serious reservations about the validity of MUQ as a measure of investment efficiency, the credibility of the causal architecture implied (despite disclaimers), and the appropriateness of the China-US comparison. The paper is best understood as an ambitious descriptive exercise with a novel framing, but the framing itself contains conceptual problems that, if unresolved, undermine the paper's core claims. The manuscript currently reads as though the descriptive patterns are self-evidently important, when in fact the interpretation of those patterns depends critically on assumptions that are not adequately defended.

**Competitiveness for Nature**: The question is genuinely important and the scope is impressive. However, the conceptual foundations of MUQ are insufficiently rigorous for a paper that aspires to reshape how we think about urban investment. A Nature referee panel will include someone who knows Pritchett (2000) and Hsieh-Klenow (2009), and that person will raise many of the issues I raise below. In its current form, this is a strong paper for a specialty journal (Journal of Urban Economics, Journal of Development Economics) but needs substantial conceptual tightening for Nature.

## 3. MUQ as an Investment Efficiency Measure: Detailed Assessment

### 3.1 What MUQ actually measures

MUQ is defined as Delta-V / I, where Delta-V is the change in market value of the urban housing stock and I is gross fixed-asset investment. This is conceptually related to Tobin's marginal q (Hayashi, 1982), but the analogy breaks down in several important ways.

**Problem 1: Delta-V conflates investment returns with asset price movements.** In corporate finance, marginal q = dV/dI measures the market's valuation of the marginal unit of installed capital. For this to be a valid efficiency metric, V must reflect the present discounted value of future cash flows generated by the marginal investment. In the urban context, Delta-V captures changes in housing prices times existing stock -- which can move for reasons entirely unrelated to investment efficiency (monetary policy, credit conditions, speculative dynamics, expectation shifts). The authors acknowledge this in the discussion ("more than 90% of the estimated 5.3 GtCO2 falls in 2021-2024 when asset price declines dominate Delta-V"), but do not adequately grapple with its implications for the entire paper. If MUQ is primarily driven by asset price cycles rather than investment productivity, then the Simpson's paradox, the sign reversal, and the carbon estimate all inherit this problem.

**Comparison with ICOR**: The Incremental Capital-Output Ratio (ICOR = I / Delta-GDP) measures how much investment is needed to generate a unit of output growth. ICOR has well-known problems (Easterly, 1999), but at least GDP growth is a flow measure that reflects production. MUQ's numerator (Delta-V) is a change in asset stock valuation, which can be positive even when no productive investment occurs (pure price appreciation) and negative even when investment is highly productive (market correction).

**Comparison with Pritchett's CUDIE**: Pritchett (2000) showed that cumulated depreciated investment effort (CUDIE) is not capital -- that the PIM assumption of constant efficiency means official capital stocks are fantasies in countries with poor institutions. The authors cite Pritchett approvingly but then construct V using housing prices that embed exactly the kind of institutional quality variation Pritchett warned about. In well-governed cities, housing prices capitalise amenity value, public goods, and institutional quality. In poorly governed cities, they do not. MUQ differences across cities therefore partly reflect institutional quality differences, not investment efficiency differences. This is not a bug to be acknowledged in a limitations paragraph -- it is the central identification problem.

**Comparison with PIMI**: Dabla-Norris et al. (2012) constructed a Public Investment Management Index precisely because outcome-based measures like ICOR confound investment quality with other factors. Their insight was that you need to measure the process (institutions governing project appraisal, selection, implementation, evaluation) to assess efficiency. MUQ goes in the opposite direction -- it measures an outcome (asset price change) and attributes it to investment efficiency. This is valid only under strong assumptions about efficient capitalisation that are implausible in exactly the countries where the paper claims MUQ is most informative.

**Problem 2: Numerator-denominator mismatch.** V measures residential housing value. I measures total fixed-asset investment (including infrastructure, industrial, commercial, and residential). The numerator captures returns to one asset class; the denominator captures investment in all asset classes. If a city invests heavily in transport infrastructure that raises housing values in neighbouring cities but not locally, its MUQ will be low even though the investment was socially efficient. Conversely, a city that restricts supply to inflate housing prices will have high MUQ with zero productive investment.

**Problem 3: The MUQ < 1 threshold is not a natural break-even.** The paper treats MUQ = 1 as the "break-even" point. But Delta-V / I = 1 means that the change in housing asset value equals total fixed-asset investment. There is no economic reason why this should be the break-even. Investment generates returns through many channels: GDP growth, employment, tax revenue, amenity value, productivity spillovers. Housing price appreciation is one channel, and often a minor one for public infrastructure. The authors acknowledge that "MUQ does not capture the social value of public goods," but this caveat undermines the entire carbon cost calculation, which is premised on MUQ < 1 indicating "value-eroding" investment.

### 3.2 What MUQ does well

To be fair, MUQ has genuine advantages:

- It is constructible from widely available data (unlike PIMI, which requires institutional surveys).
- It captures the asset-side information that ICOR misses (ICOR ignores whether investment creates wealth or debt).
- The sign reversal between China and the US is a genuinely informative stylised fact that survives alternative specifications.
- The Simpson's paradox framing, while overblown, does highlight a real compositional issue in aggregate investment statistics.

### 3.3 Recommended improvements

1. **Reframe MUQ explicitly as a "housing market signal" rather than an "investment efficiency" measure.** MUQ captures how well investment translates into housing asset values. This is informative but narrower than "efficiency."
2. **Construct a GDP-based MUQ (Delta-GDP / I = 1/ICOR) as a parallel series** and show whether the Simpson's paradox holds. If it does, the finding is more robust. If it doesn't, that tells us something about asset price dynamics versus productivity.
3. **Acknowledge the Hayashi conditions explicitly.** Hayashi (1982) showed marginal q = average q only under constant returns to scale and competitive markets. Neither holds in Chinese urban land markets. State the implications.

## 4. Causal Inference Audit

### 4.1 The "descriptive" framing is inconsistent with the narrative

The paper states: "MUQ is a descriptive measure of investment outcomes, not an identification strategy for causal mechanisms." And: "we do not claim to identify why returns erode, only that they do." This is commendably honest. But the paper then proceeds to:

- Invoke "supply-driven vs demand-driven regimes" as the explanation for the sign reversal (this is a causal claim about institutional mechanisms).
- Call the scaling gap the "theoretical engine" (engines cause things).
- Describe investment flowing "against the scaling gradient" as "producing below-cost returns" (this is a causal statement about the effect of misallocation).
- Calculate carbon costs of "value-eroding investment" (this presumes the investment caused the erosion, or at minimum that the investment was the appropriate counterfactual target).

This tension between stated epistemic modesty and actual rhetorical practice is the paper's most serious weakness. A Nature referee will notice this immediately. The solution is not more disclaimers -- it is to either commit to a causal identification strategy or genuinely restrict claims to description.

### 4.2 The Three Red Lines DID

I appreciate the authors' honesty about the limitations. The parallel trends test is marginal (F = 2.82, p = 0.093), and the placebo test is significant (beta = 0.067, p < 0.001). These are not merely "limitations" -- they are diagnostic failures. A significant placebo coefficient means that RE-dependent cities were already on different trajectories before the policy. The DID is invalid for causal inference.

The dose-response pattern (Q2: -0.045, Q3: -0.095, Q4: -0.136) is suggestive of a real effect, but the linear trend test is itself marginal (F = 3.017, p = 0.082). The event-study pre-treatment coefficients, which are the gold standard for parallel trends validation, are not reported -- only the joint F-test is given.

**Recommendation**: Either (a) drop the DID entirely and save it for a separate paper where you can do it justice with better identification (synthetic control, regression discontinuity at the RE-dependence threshold), or (b) report the full event-study coefficients and honestly characterise this as a "failed" natural experiment that nevertheless provides suggestive evidence. Option (b) is what the current text does, but the placement in a Nature paper gives it more weight than it deserves.

### 4.3 Cross-country correlations

The Simpson's paradox is based on Spearman rank correlations between MUQ and urbanisation rate within income groups. The effect sizes are small (rho = -0.099 to -0.150). These are bivariate correlations without any controls for confounders that are obviously important:

- **Terms of trade shocks**: Commodity-exporting countries in the UMI group (Russia, Brazil, Saudi Arabia) experience asset price booms and busts driven by oil/commodity prices, not urbanisation dynamics.
- **Financial crises**: The Asian financial crisis, GFC, and European debt crisis affected MUQ through asset price channels, not investment efficiency channels.
- **Institutional quality**: Within the UMI group, Turkey and Brazil have very different institutional environments from China and Mexico. Pooling them treats institutional variation as noise.
- **Demographic transitions**: Countries at different stages of demographic transition have different investment needs regardless of urbanisation rate.

The leave-one-out robustness is reassuring for outlier sensitivity but does not address omitted variable bias. A panel regression with country fixed effects and time-varying controls (terms of trade, financial openness, institutional quality, demographic dependency ratio) would be far more informative than bivariate Spearman correlations.

## 5. Major Concerns

### M1. The China-US comparison conflates measurement differences with institutional differences

**The problem**: MUQ_China = Delta(Population x Price x PerCapitaArea) / FAI. MUQ_US = Delta(MedianHomeValue x HousingUnits) / (Delta-HU x LaggedPrice). These are different objects:

- China's V includes population growth mechanically (V = Pop x Price x Area/capita). If population grows, V grows even with constant prices and per-capita area. The US V does not have this property in the same way.
- China's I is total FAI (all sectors). The US "I" is imputed from housing unit changes -- it captures residential construction only. This means China's MUQ has a much larger denominator, biasing it downward relative to the US.
- The sign reversal (China beta = -2.23, US beta = +2.75) could partly reflect this definitional asymmetry: in China, high-FAI cities are building infrastructure (which doesn't show up in V), while in the US, high-construction cities are building houses (which directly enters V).

The authors attempt a unified specification (Delta-V/GDP) and find the contrast holds (China beta = -0.37, US beta = +1.78). This is helpful but the magnitudes are much closer, and -0.37 is barely significant (p = 0.019). This suggests that a substantial portion of the sign reversal is indeed definitional.

**Literature reference**: Hsieh and Moretti (2019, AJR) show that spatial misallocation of housing construction in the US (building in low-productivity cities instead of high-productivity ones) costs 36% of aggregate growth. Their framework would predict a negative MUQ-construction relationship even in the US if construction is measured correctly and includes misallocated investment. The positive US sign may reflect reverse causality (cities with rising prices attract more construction) rather than demand-driven efficiency.

**Required action**: Present a table showing exactly which components of V and I differ between the two countries, and run the sign-reversal test on identically defined variables. If this is impossible with available data, state this as a fundamental limitation rather than a finding.

### M2. The Simpson's Paradox may be an artefact of asset price cyclicality

**The problem**: MUQ uses Delta-V in the numerator. For developing countries, housing price data from PWT capital stocks (rnna) are notoriously unreliable (Feenstra, Inklaar, and Timmer, 2015, AER). More importantly, asset prices in developing countries are highly cyclical and correlated with commodity prices, capital flows, and exchange rates. When a lower-middle-income country urbanises from 30% to 50%, it often does so during a period of commodity boom (think Brazil 2003-2013) followed by bust. The MUQ decline from 9.88 to 1.15 across urbanisation stages could reflect nothing more than the fact that countries at higher urbanisation stages in 2019 (the PWT endpoint) were measured during the post-commodity-boom period, while countries at lower urbanisation stages were measured during the boom.

**Test**: Compute MUQ using a 5-year moving average of Delta-V to smooth price cycles. If the Simpson's paradox survives, it is more robust. If it disappears, it is an artefact of price cyclicality.

**Literature reference**: Kose, Prasad, and Terrones (2009, JDE) document that financial integration increases asset price cyclicality in developing countries. This is exactly the confound that threatens the MUQ-urbanisation correlation.

### M3. China's FAI data are structurally unreliable post-2017

**The problem**: The data sources documentation acknowledges that FAI data post-2017 are estimated from published growth rates due to the discontinuation of the total-society FAI series, and that the statistical calibre changed in 2018. This means that the FAI series -- the denominator of MUQ -- has a structural break in 2017-2018. The national MUQ series spans 1998-2024, and more than 90% of the carbon cost falls in 2021-2024, precisely when FAI data are estimated rather than observed.

This is a first-order problem. The carbon cost calculation rests entirely on the post-2020 MUQ trajectory, which in turn rests on estimated FAI data applied to a housing market in free fall. The combination of an estimated denominator and a collapsing numerator could easily produce artefactual MUQ < 0.

**Literature reference**: Holz (2014, China Economic Review) provides extensive documentation of Chinese statistical system deficiencies. Wu (2014, Brookings) specifically addresses investment data reliability.

**Required action**: (a) Report MUQ separately for the pre-2017 period (observed data) and post-2017 period (estimated data), with explicit uncertainty bands for the latter. (b) Use an alternative investment series (e.g., cement production, steel consumption) as a physical proxy to validate the FAI trajectory. (c) Conduct sensitivity analysis where post-2017 FAI is varied by +/- 20%.

### M4. The "scaling gap" contains a mechanical component that is inadequately addressed

**The problem**: The authors acknowledge this in the limitations: "V is constructed using population-weighted terms, so the superlinear V-population relationship partly reflects measurement construction." This is more serious than a limitation -- it is a potential invalidation of the core theoretical mechanism. If V = Pop x Price x Area/capita, then log(V) = log(Pop) + log(Price) + log(Area/capita). The regression log(V) = a + beta x log(Pop) will return beta > 1 whenever log(Price) or log(Area/capita) is positively correlated with log(Pop), which is virtually guaranteed by the housing price-population gradient. The "scaling gap" may be nothing more than the well-documented fact that housing prices are higher in larger cities (Glaeser, Gyourko, and Saks, 2005, NBER).

**Test**: Regress log(Price) and log(Area/capita) separately on log(Pop) to decompose the scaling exponent. Report what fraction of beta_V > 1 comes from each component. If it is entirely driven by the price-population gradient, the "scaling gap" adds no information beyond what we already know about urban housing prices.

### M5. Missing literature engagement

The paper's reference list is strikingly thin for a paper that claims to provide a new framework for understanding investment efficiency across countries. Key omissions:

1. **Hsieh and Klenow (2009, QJE)**: "Misallocation and Manufacturing TFP in China and India." The canonical paper on misallocation -- provides a natural benchmark for understanding MUQ variation across cities.
2. **Restuccia and Rogerson (2008, RES)**: "Policy Distortions and Aggregate Productivity with Heterogeneous Establishments." The theoretical foundation for thinking about how policy-driven investment misallocation reduces aggregate efficiency.
3. **Brandt, Van Biesebroeck, and Zhang (2012, AER)**: "Creative Accounting or Creative Destruction? Firm-level Productivity Growth in Chinese Manufacturing." Directly relevant to understanding Chinese investment data quality.
4. **Easterly (1999, WB Economic Review)**: "The Ghost of Financing Gap." The classic critique of ICOR-type measures and their policy misuse -- directly relevant to MUQ's potential misuse.
5. **Hsieh and Moretti (2019, AJR)**: Spatial misallocation of housing in the US -- the closest antecedent to the paper's argument in the US context.
6. **Glaeser and Gyourko (2018, JEP)**: "The Economic Implications of Housing Supply." Essential for understanding why housing investment responds differently to price signals in different institutional environments.
7. **Bai, Hsieh, and Song (2016, Brookings)**: "The Long Shadow of a Fiscal Expansion." Directly relevant to understanding why Chinese local government investment may be inefficient.

With only 18 references (Nature allows 50), the paper gives the impression of not being situated in the relevant literature. This is a serious weakness for Nature, where referees expect authors to demonstrate command of the field.

### M6. The carbon cost estimate lacks economic logic

**The problem**: The paper defines "excess investment" as I(t) x max(0, 1 - MUQ(t)). This means that when MUQ = 0.5, half of all investment is classified as "excess." But this calculation assumes that the counterfactual is zero investment when MUQ < 1. In reality:

- Some investment with MUQ < 1 produces essential public goods (hospitals, schools, water systems) whose social returns exceed private asset returns.
- Some investment with MUQ < 1 is maintenance investment necessary to prevent capital depreciation, which would generate even lower MUQ if deferred.
- The "excess" framing assumes the planner can perfectly calibrate investment to the MUQ = 1 threshold, which ignores indivisibilities, coordination failures, and option values.

The 5.3 GtCO2 figure, prominently featured in the abstract and title framing, rests on an economically nonsensical counterfactual. No development economist would accept that all investment with private asset return below cost is "excess." This is the Achilles' heel of the climate framing.

**Required action**: Either (a) redefine "excess" using a more defensible counterfactual (e.g., investment in cities below the 25th percentile of MUQ, compared to the median), or (b) present the carbon number as "carbon embodied in below-unity-return investment" without the normative "excess" framing, and let readers draw their own conclusions.

## 6. Minor Concerns

### m1. The mean-field model in Box 1 is ad hoc
The model MUQ_k = mu_k - gamma x u_k + epsilon has no micro-foundations. It is a reduced-form description of the pattern the paper claims to document, not a theoretical explanation. Calling it a "framework" overstates its contribution. It generates "testable predictions" that are restatements of the empirical patterns.

### m2. Winsorisation at 1%/99% may be insufficient for MUQ
MUQ can be extremely volatile (small I in the denominator creates explosions). The paper should report sensitivity to 5%/95% winsorisation and to trimming (removing outliers entirely rather than capping them).

### m3. The "82.2% of cities" statistic is misleading
This statistic (82.2% of cities have MUQ < 1 in the 2016 cross-section) is heavily influenced by the large number of small cities. Weighted by population or GDP, the figure would be very different and more policy-relevant.

### m4. Fixed-income classification for the Simpson's paradox
Using the "most recent" income classification to assign countries to groups means that a country that was LMI in 1980 and UMI in 2020 is classified as UMI for its entire trajectory. This biases the within-UMI MUQ-urbanisation relationship because the country's early observations (when it was actually LMI with lower urbanisation and possibly higher MUQ) are attributed to the UMI group.

### m5. The Spearman correlations are very weak
rho = -0.099 to -0.150 explains less than 2.3% of variance. While statistically significant, the practical significance is minimal. The paper should discuss effect sizes more carefully.

### m6. The Discussion's final paragraph is hyperbolic
"One of the largest misallocations of physical capital in modern history has been hiding in plain sight" -- this claim requires far stronger evidence than bivariate correlations and a fragile MUQ metric. Hsieh and Klenow (2009) estimated manufacturing TFP losses of 30-50% from misallocation in China -- a finding based on firm-level data and a rigorous model. The present paper's evidence does not support claims of comparable magnitude.

### m7. Missing event-study coefficients
The DID section reports joint F-tests for pre-treatment coefficients but not the individual year-by-year coefficients. Best practice (Freyaldenhoven et al., 2019; Roth, 2022) requires plotting all pre- and post-treatment coefficients to assess parallel trends visually and statistically.

## 7. Recommendation

**Major Revision**, with a strong caution that the revision must address conceptual foundations, not just add robustness checks.

The paper tackles an important question with an ambitious empirical scope. But the core metric (MUQ) has fundamental conceptual problems that the current text acknowledges in passing but does not resolve. The paper's contribution is best characterised as: (a) documenting a compositional effect (Simpson's paradox) in aggregate investment statistics, which is a real insight; (b) constructing a novel cross-national dataset of urban investment returns, which is a valuable public good; and (c) identifying a suggestive sign reversal between institutional regimes, which is interesting but not conclusive.

For Nature, the conceptual bar is higher than "novel and suggestive." The paper needs to either:

1. **Strengthen the theoretical foundations** of MUQ by explicitly engaging with the corporate finance literature on marginal q (Abel and Eberly, 1994; Erickson and Whited, 2000), showing that the Hayashi conditions hold approximately in the urban context, or providing alternative micro-foundations.
2. **Provide a credible identification strategy** for at least one of the three findings -- even if the other two remain descriptive. The Three Red Lines DID is the natural candidate, but it needs much more work (synthetic control, regression kink design, or at minimum a convincing pre-trends analysis).
3. **Reframe the contribution** more modestly as a measurement innovation and descriptive documentation, dropping the causal language ("engine," "supply-driven vs demand-driven regimes") and the hyperbolic concluding claims.

Without at least one of these three changes, I cannot recommend acceptance at Nature. With the first and third changes, this could be a strong Nature paper. With all three, it would be exceptional.
